Is Your Website ADA Compliant? A Small Business Owner's Guide (2026)

Updated March 2026 · 8 min read

⚠️ The numbers are alarming: Over 8,600 ADA website lawsuits were filed in 2025 — a 37% increase from the year before. The average out-of-court settlement? $30,000. And 94.8% of websites fail basic accessibility standards.

If you own a small business with a website, you could be a target. ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) website compliance isn't just about doing the right thing — it's about protecting your business from lawsuits that can cost tens of thousands of dollars.

Here's what you need to know, and how to check your site in 30 seconds.

What Is ADA Website Compliance?

The ADA requires businesses to make their goods and services accessible to people with disabilities. Courts have increasingly ruled that this extends to websites — your website is considered a "place of public accommodation."

The standard most referenced is WCAG 2.1 (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines), which covers things like:

  • Screen reader compatibility for blind and visually impaired users
  • Keyboard navigation for people who can't use a mouse
  • Color contrast for people with low vision
  • Captions and transcripts for deaf and hard-of-hearing users
  • Clear, simple navigation for cognitive disabilities

10 Things to Check Right Now

1. Image Alt Text

Every image on your site needs descriptive alt text so screen readers can describe them to blind users. This is the #1 most common accessibility failure.

2. Color Contrast

Text must have sufficient contrast against its background. WCAG requires a minimum ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text. Gray text on white? Probably failing.

3. Keyboard Navigation

Can you navigate your entire site using only the Tab and Enter keys? If not, users who can't use a mouse are locked out.

4. Form Labels

Every form field (name, email, phone) needs a proper label — not just placeholder text. Screen readers need labels to tell users what to type where.

5. Heading Structure

Use proper heading tags (H1, H2, H3) in logical order. Don't skip from H1 to H4. Screen readers use headings to navigate page structure.

6. Link Text

"Click here" and "Read more" tell a screen reader nothing. Links should describe where they go: "View our menu" or "Book an appointment."

7. Video Captions

Any video on your site needs captions. Auto-generated captions from YouTube are a start, but they're often inaccurate and may not satisfy compliance requirements.

8. Mobile Responsiveness

Your site must be usable on mobile devices. Text should be readable without zooming, buttons large enough to tap, and content shouldn't require horizontal scrolling.

9. Error Messages

When a user makes an error on a form, the error message must clearly explain what went wrong and how to fix it. Color alone isn't enough (colorblind users can't see red).

10. Page Language

Your HTML needs a language attribute (<html lang="en">) so screen readers know what language to use. Simple fix, often missed.

What Happens If You Get Sued?

The typical ADA website lawsuit follows a pattern:

  1. A plaintiff (often represented by a serial-filing law firm) tests your site for accessibility failures
  2. They file a lawsuit claiming they couldn't access your goods/services
  3. You receive a demand letter offering to settle for $5,000–$50,000
  4. Fighting it in court costs $10,000–$100,000+ in legal fees — even if you win
  5. Most businesses settle for $20,000–$40,000 and agree to fix their site

The cruel irony: fixing your site beforehand usually costs under $5,000. The lawsuit costs 10x more.

How to Protect Your Business

Prevention is dramatically cheaper than defense:

  • Audit your site now — identify accessibility issues before a lawyer does
  • Fix the basics first — alt text, contrast, keyboard nav, and form labels cover 80% of issues
  • Add an accessibility statement — shows good faith effort
  • Test regularly — accessibility can break when you update your site
  • Consider an accessibility widget — tools like UserWay or accessiBe add basic features, though they're not a complete solution
