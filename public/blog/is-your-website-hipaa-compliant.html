Is Your Website HIPAA Compliant?

Published March 24, 2026 · 7 min read · By Summit Web Audit

If you run a dental practice, chiropractic office, physical therapy clinic, med spa, or any healthcare-adjacent business — your website probably needs to comply with HIPAA. And there's a good chance it doesn't.

$1.5M+

The average HIPAA violation fine for small healthcare businesses. Even a single complaint about your website can trigger an investigation by the Office for Civil Rights (OCR).

HIPAA (the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) doesn't just apply to patient records and billing. It applies to any system that collects, transmits, or stores protected health information (PHI) — and your website almost certainly does.

Does HIPAA Apply to My Website?

If your website does any of the following, HIPAA likely applies:

  • Contact forms where patients describe symptoms or conditions
  • Online appointment scheduling
  • Patient intake forms or health questionnaires
  • Patient portals or login areas
  • Live chat where health information might be discussed
  • Email collection for appointment reminders or newsletters

Even a simple "Tell us about your concern" textarea on a contact form can collect PHI if a patient describes their health condition.

The 7 Most Common Website HIPAA Violations

1. No SSL Certificate (or Mixed Content)

HIPAA requires encryption of PHI in transit. If your website doesn't use HTTPS on every page — especially forms — you're violating the Security Rule. Mixed content (loading some resources over HTTP) also counts.

✅ How to Check

Look for the padlock icon in your browser's address bar. Click it — does it say "Connection is secure"? If you see any warnings, you have a problem.

2. Contact Forms Without Encryption

Your contact form might use HTTPS for the page, but where does the data go? If submissions are emailed to a regular Gmail or Yahoo account (not a HIPAA-compliant email service), that's a violation.

3. Google Analytics Tracking PHI

Standard Google Analytics can capture URL parameters, search queries, and form data. If a patient's name, condition, or appointment details appear in any tracked URL, you're storing PHI on Google's servers — which aren't covered by a BAA (Business Associate Agreement).

⚠️ This Is More Common Than You Think

In 2023, the HHS warned that tracking technologies like Google Analytics and Meta Pixel on healthcare websites may constitute impermissible disclosures of PHI. Multiple health systems faced lawsuits for exactly this.

4. No Business Associate Agreements (BAAs)

Every third-party service that handles PHI needs a BAA. This includes:

  • Your web hosting provider
  • Your email service (if patients email you)
  • Your appointment scheduling tool
  • Your CRM or patient management software
  • Your live chat provider

If any of these vendors don't offer a BAA, you can't use them for anything involving patient information.

5. Missing Privacy Policy

HIPAA requires a Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP). Your website should have a clear, accessible privacy policy that explains how you collect, use, and protect health information. Many small practice websites either don't have one or use a generic template that doesn't address HIPAA.

6. No Security Headers

HIPAA's Security Rule requires "reasonable and appropriate" technical safeguards. Security headers like HSTS, Content Security Policy, and X-Frame-Options help protect your site from common attacks. Most small practice websites have none of these.

7. Outdated Software

Running an old version of WordPress, an outdated plugin, or an unpatched CMS is a security risk — and a HIPAA risk. The Security Rule requires you to protect against "reasonably anticipated threats" to ePHI.

What to Do Right Now

  1. Audit your website for SSL, security headers, and form handling
  2. Review all third-party tools — do they offer BAAs?
  3. Check your forms — where does submitted data go?
  4. Update your privacy policy to include HIPAA-required disclosures
  5. Remove or configure analytics to exclude PHI from tracking
