Is Your Medical Practice Website HIPAA Compliant? A 2026 Checklist

By Steve · March 24, 2026 · 8 min read

If you run a medical practice, dental office, chiropractic clinic, or any healthcare business — your website is almost certainly handling protected health information (PHI) in ways you haven't thought about.

And if it's not HIPAA compliant, you're exposed to fines of $100 to $50,000 per violation, up to $1.5 million per year.

⚠️ The scary part: Most violations come from websites and online forms, not hacked medical records. A simple contact form that asks "What are your symptoms?" is collecting PHI — and if it's not encrypted and properly handled, it's a HIPAA violation.

What Counts as PHI on a Website?

Protected Health Information isn't just medical records. On a website, PHI includes:

  • Contact forms that ask about health conditions, symptoms, or reason for visit
  • Appointment booking forms that collect patient names + health-related info
  • Patient portals or login areas
  • Live chat where patients discuss health concerns
  • Email addresses collected in the context of healthcare services
  • IP addresses combined with any of the above (yes, really)

The 10-Point Website HIPAA Checklist

Run through this checklist for your practice's website. If you can't check every box, you have work to do.

🔒 Security Basics

  • SSL/HTTPS on every page — Not just the homepage. Every single page, especially forms, must be served over HTTPS. Check for the padlock icon.
  • HSTS header enabled — Forces browsers to always use HTTPS. Without it, a visitor could accidentally load the HTTP version and transmit data unencrypted.
  • Content Security Policy (CSP) — Prevents malicious scripts from running on your site and stealing form data.
  • Secure form handling — All forms that collect any patient info must submit data over encrypted connections to HIPAA-compliant servers.

📋 Forms & Data Collection

  • No PHI in URLs or query strings — If your form puts patient data in the URL bar (like ?name=John&condition=diabetes), that's a violation. Use POST requests, not GET.
  • HIPAA-compliant form processor — Free form tools (Google Forms, Typeform, basic WordPress plugins) are NOT HIPAA compliant. You need a BAA (Business Associate Agreement) with your form provider.
  • Data encryption at rest — Patient data stored on your server must be encrypted, not sitting in a plain text database.

📊 Analytics & Tracking

  • Google Analytics configured correctly — Standard GA4 can collect IP addresses alongside page visits, which becomes PHI in a healthcare context. You need IP anonymization enabled at minimum.
  • No third-party tracking pixels on health-related pages — Facebook Pixel, Google Ads remarketing, and similar tools on pages where patients book appointments or fill forms may share PHI with third parties.
  • Cookie consent — While not strictly HIPAA, it's best practice and may be required under state laws. Inform visitors about what you're tracking.

💡 Quick test: Open your website and look at the address bar. If you see "http://" instead of "https://" on ANY page — especially pages with forms — that's an immediate compliance issue. Fix it today.

The Most Common Violations We See

After auditing hundreds of small business websites, here are the HIPAA-related issues we find most often on medical practice sites:

  1. Contact forms on HTTP pages — The form looks fine, but the page isn't encrypted. Every submission is sent in plain text.
  2. Missing security headers — No HSTS, no CSP, no X-Frame-Options. This is like leaving your office door unlocked overnight.
  3. Google Forms for patient intake — Google Forms is NOT HIPAA compliant. Google does not sign BAAs for their free products.
  4. Facebook Pixel on booking pages — You're literally sending patient appointment data to Facebook. This has led to real lawsuits.
  5. Outdated WordPress plugins — Old plugins with known vulnerabilities are a breach waiting to happen.

What To Do Next

If your website failed any of the checks above, here's your priority order:

  1. Enable HTTPS everywhere (today — free with Let's Encrypt)
  2. Add security headers (HSTS, CSP, X-Frame-Options — your web host can help)
  3. Audit your forms — Replace non-compliant form processors with HIPAA-compliant ones (JotForm HIPAA, Formstack, or custom solutions with BAAs)
  4. Review your analytics — Enable IP anonymization, remove tracking pixels from health-related pages
  5. Get a professional audit — A comprehensive review catches things you'll miss doing it yourself

HIPAA Fines Are No Joke

The Office for Civil Rights (OCR) has been increasingly aggressive about enforcement, especially for small practices that assume they're "too small to be noticed."

  • Tier 1: $100–$50,000 per violation (didn't know about it)
  • Tier 2: $1,000–$50,000 per violation (reasonable cause)
  • Tier 3: $10,000–$50,000 per violation (willful neglect, corrected)
  • Tier 4: $50,000+ per violation (willful neglect, not corrected)

Annual maximum: $1.5 million per violation category.

Even Tier 1 adds up fast. If your contact form has been collecting unencrypted PHI for a year, every form submission could be a separate violation.
